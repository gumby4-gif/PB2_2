What Is a
Rain Garden?

A shallow, planted depression that captures roof and pavement runoff, filters it through native roots, and returns it slowly to the ground — instead of rushing it into storm drains and streams.

The Basics

More than a pretty garden —
a working system

When rain hits a conventional yard, it collects on rooftops, rolls across driveways, and rushes into storm drains — carrying fertilizers, pet waste, motor oil, and sediment straight into local streams. In Northern Virginia, that runoff reaches the Potomac watershed within hours.

A rain garden interrupts that journey. Positioned to receive water from a downspout or low-lying area, its shallow bowl holds runoff for 24–48 hours while native plant roots and specially amended soil filter pollutants and slowly recharge groundwater below.

"A well-placed rain garden can absorb 30–40% more water than a conventional lawn of the same size."

30–40%

Compared to a conventional turf lawn of equal size, a rain garden filters significantly more stormwater before it can become runoff.

A well-designed rain garden drains completely within 24–48 hours after a storm — preventing standing water and mosquito habitat.

10×
Deeper root systems

Native Virginia plants develop roots 6–12 feet deep — far deeper than turf grass — creating natural channels for water to move through soil.

0
Supplemental irrigation

Once established (typically after 1–2 seasons), a native rain garden requires no additional watering — it is sustained entirely by rainfall.

Siting & Design

Where does a rain garden go?

The location matters as much as the planting. A rain garden placed too close to a foundation can cause water intrusion; sited correctly, it draws water away from the house and slows it before it leaves your property.

01

Find the downspout or low point

Rain gardens are most effective when fed by a single downspout or positioned at the natural low point of a yard where water already collects after storms.

02

Observe after a heavy rain

Walk your yard 30 minutes after a significant storm. Note where water pools, where it flows, and where the ground stays dry. That map tells you where your rain garden wants to be.

03

Keep 10 feet from the foundation

Place the rain garden at least 10 feet from your home's foundation and away from septic systems or utility lines. It should sit in full or partial sun — most native rain garden plants perform best with 4+ hours of direct light.

04

Test your drainage rate

Dig a hole 12 inches deep and fill it with water. If it drains within 24 hours, you have suitable soil. Clay-heavy soils — common across Northern Virginia's Piedmont — benefit from an amended planting mix of 50–60% sand, 20–30% compost, and native topsoil.

05

Size it to your catchment area

A general rule: the rain garden should be roughly 20–30% of the surface area that drains into it. A 1,000 sq ft roof section typically calls for a 200–300 sq ft rain garden.

Plant Selection

Virginia natives that thrive in wet-dry cycles

The genius of a rain garden is that it asks plants to tolerate two opposing conditions — temporary flooding after storms, and dry periods between them. Native Virginia species evolved to do exactly this. Their deep root systems hold soil during floods and seek moisture during droughts.

Plant selection should account for the three zones of a rain garden: the center (wettest, tolerates temporary inundation), the middle slopes (moist but well-drained), and the outer edges (drier, matching your surrounding landscape). Layering plants across these zones extends bloom time through the season while supporting a full range of pollinators — from early spring Virginia Bluebells to late-summer Joe Pye that monarchs depend on during migration.

Is It Right for You?

A rain garden might be the answer if —

You notice water pooling in the same spot after every storm. Your downspouts drain directly to pavement or lawn. Your local municipality offers stormwater credits or rebates (many Virginia counties do). Or you simply want a garden that does meaningful ecological work while requiring less maintenance than traditional plantings over time.

What a rain garden is not: a pond, a bog, or a wet area that stays saturated. If designed and planted correctly, it is a beautiful, seasonally dynamic garden that happens to solve a real problem — one storm at a time.
