Project Beecher High School

Status: Completed · Owner: Erin Amanda Schulz · Bunny Hill Native Landscapes

Overview

The Beecher High School Outdoor Classroom is a place-based education initiative developed in partnership with Discovering PLACE — a collective of Flint-area K–12 schools, University of Michigan-Flint faculty and staff, community organizations, and parents. Working together with Beecher Middle/High School, a Flint public school, the design team created a place-based outdoor classroom and developed a corresponding implementation plan, best practices guide, and integrated curriculum. The overarching goal is to strengthen the presence of place-based education at Beecher and to document the process so that the experience can serve as a framework for other schools seeking to incorporate place-based learning into their own educational practices.

The site is organized around eight distinct zones: a Native Plant & Butterfly Spiral with informal seating and boulders; an Outdoor Meeting Space with wood and tire seating beneath shade sails and native prairie plantings; a Miniature Orchard of apple and cherry trees; an Entrance Garden featuring an arbor, native plantings, and educational signage along a decomposed granite path; a Vegetable & Fruit Garden with raised beds, communal beds, compost bin, tool shed, and rain barrels; a Trail Extension to a Pond boardwalk through existing forest; a Native Bush Hedge lawn edge of Gro-Low Fragrant Sumac; and a Path to Water Source marked by a commemorative boulder. Material choices throughout — recycled tires as planters and seating, wooden boardwalk, hoop houses, permeable concrete pavers, and decomposed granite — evoke an easygoing, imaginative atmosphere while extending a nod to Flint's long history in the state automobile industry.

Design Goals

Integrate curriculum across Art, Language Arts, Math, Science, and Social Studies through outdoor learning experiences · Maximize maintenance, safety, and accessibility for students, staff, and community volunteers · Minimize site and habitat disruption by working with existing topography, trees, and site features · Incorporate existing site elements including the pond, baseball field, and forest edge · Appeal to a variety of ages across the Beecher Middle and High School campus · Feature local culture and environment by referencing Flint's automotive heritage and native Michigan prairie and butterfly species · Select native plants that provide sanctuary for insects, birds, and small mammals, reduce soil erosion, and increase water infiltration · Create student-built educational signage teaching local visitors about history, habitat restoration, and the outdoor classroom's learning opportunities.

Current Status

Design and curriculum development are complete. Deliverables include the full seven-slide presentation package covering the Conceptual Site Plan, Materials & Character, Native Plant Palette, Vegetable Garden Elevation, Entrance Elevation, Outdoor Meeting Space, and Analysis of Site Context. The accompanying Growing Our Place curriculum — spanning Art, Language Arts, Math, Science, and Social Studies — provides lesson plans including "Why Do We Eat What We Eat?" and "Pollinator's Journey," both developed as products of the SNRE Master's Project at the University of Michigan. The native plant palette features thirteen Michigan species: Bee Balm, Joe-Pye Weed, Gro-Low Fragrant Sumac, Butterfly Weed, Orange Coneflower, Indian Grass, Common Milkweed, Apple Tree, Wild Strawberry, Little Blue Stem, Purple Love Grass, New England Aster, and Blazing Star.
