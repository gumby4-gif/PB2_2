Project UM Dana Garden

Status: Completed · Owner: Erin Amanda Schulz · Bunny Hill Native Landscapes

Overview

Recognizing the structural force of landscape, the Dana Garden redesign for the Samuel T. Dana Building at the University of Michigan strives to emphasize the gradations of the existing topography. The design utilizes a mixture of both native and non-native plants at a variety of heights and textures, with plant choice and placement centered around the site's dry sandy loam soil and available sunlight. These conditions are used to recreate areas reminiscent of a tall grass prairie and woodland setting. All choices within the garden revolve around making a welcoming place-based outdoor classroom that highlights the native — and some non-native — flora growing in the state of Michigan, in direct support of the School of Natural Resources and Environment curriculum.

Design Goals

Accentuate native Michigan flora in landscape design · Emphasize the gradations of the existing topography · Create a sense of privacy and sanctuary away from the bustling thoroughfare next-door · Produce an engaging sensory experience through color and texture · Tie style, texture, and plant choice into the surrounding University setting · Provide opportunities for learning in conjunction with the curriculum of the School of Natural Resources & Environment.

Current Status

Design and installation are complete. The planting plan features species including Fragrant Sumac, Witch Hazel, White and Pink Astilbe, Hosta, Woodland Phlox, Wild Ginger, Fern Sedge, New England Aster, Switchgrass, Russian Sage, Purple Love Grass, Blue Oat Grass, Butterfly Weed, Autumn Joy Sedum, Creeping Juniper, and Catnint, among others. The outdoor classroom seating area with timber decking has been installed, and seasonal diagrams document the garden's character across spring, summer, fall, and winter.
